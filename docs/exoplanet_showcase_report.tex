\begin{abstract}
The radial-velocity (RV) method remains one of the foundational techniques for
detecting and characterizing extrasolar planets. We present a compact,
reproducible pipeline for estimating the orbital parameters of a single
companion from Doppler time series. The forward model is the standard
Keplerian RV curve parameterized by semi-amplitude $K$, period $P$,
eccentricity $e$, argument of periastron $\omega$, time of periastron $t_0$,
and systemic velocity $\gamma$. Parameters are recovered with a global
differential-evolution search followed by local nonlinear least-squares
refinement. From the fitted amplitude and an assumed stellar mass we derive a
minimum planet mass and orbital semi-major axis under the edge-on
inclination limit. Synthetic experiments recover planted values of $K$ and $P$
to within a few percent at residual RMS of $\sim 2\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$.
Application to four public NASA Exoplanet Archive radial-velocity series (51~Peg, HD~209458, 70~Vir, and HD~3651) recovers literature-consistent single-planet solutions, including the short-period nearly circular orbit of 51~Peg~b ($P \approx 4.23\,\mathrm{d}$, $K \approx 56.1\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, $M_p\sin i \approx 0.47\,M_J$). A velocity-Verlet two-body integration is used only for post-fit orbit visualization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The discovery of 51~Pegasi~b established Doppler spectroscopy as a practical
route to detecting planets around solar-type stars
\citep{mayor1995jupiter,marcy1996planet}. In the RV method, the star and
planet orbit their common barycenter; the line-of-sight component of the
stellar motion appears as a periodic Doppler shift in the stellar spectrum.
Modern surveys and modeling frameworks treat this signal with analytic
Keplerian orbits and statistically motivated fitting procedures
\citep{wright2009efficient,cumming2004detectability,fischer2014exoplanet}.

For a single planet on a Keplerian orbit, the stellar radial velocity is fully
specified by a small set of orbital elements
\citep{murray1999solar,murray2010keplerian}. The semi-amplitude $K$ encodes
the planet-to-star mass ratio and orbital scale, while $P$ and $e$ control the
periodicity and waveform shape. Because the likelihood surface can be
multimodal---especially when eccentricity and phase are free---global
optimization or Bayesian sampling is often preferred over purely local
gradient methods \citep{ford2005quantifying,storn1997differential}.

This work implements a transparent single-planet RV estimator intended for
reproducible analysis and pedagogical demonstration. The pipeline
(i)~optionally converts Doppler shifts to radial velocities,
(ii)~estimates a dominant period with a Lomb--Scargle periodogram,
(iii)~fits a Keplerian model with differential evolution plus least-squares
polish, and
(iv)~derives physical parameters and a barycentric orbit visualization.
We validate the method on synthetic data with known ground truth and apply it
to four public NASA Exoplanet Archive radial-velocity time series
\citep{christiansen2025nea}.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Doppler shift and radial velocity}

Spectroscopic observations may be reported as a fractional Doppler shift
$z = \Delta\lambda/\lambda$. For a source with line-of-sight speed $v$
relative to the observer, the relativistic Doppler formula is
\begin{equation}
z = \sqrt{\frac{1+\beta}{1-\beta}} - 1,
\qquad
\beta = \frac{v}{c},
\end{equation}
where $c$ is the speed of light \citep{lindegren2003fundamental}. Inverting
this relation yields
\begin{equation}
v = c\,\frac{(1+z)^2 - 1}{(1+z)^2 + 1}.
\end{equation}
Throughout, positive $v$ denotes recession.

\subsection{Keplerian radial-velocity model}

We adopt the standard single-planet RV model
\citep{wright2009efficient,murray2010keplerian}:
\begin{equation}
v_r(t)
=
\gamma
+
K\Bigl[
\cos\bigl(\omega + \nu(t)\bigr)
+
e\cos\omega
\Bigr],
\label{eq:rv}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ is the systemic velocity, $K$ is the RV semi-amplitude,
$e$ is the eccentricity, $\omega$ is the argument of periastron, and
$\nu(t)$ is the true anomaly.

The true anomaly is obtained from the mean anomaly
\begin{equation}
M = \frac{2\pi}{P}\,(t - t_0)
\end{equation}
by solving Kepler's equation
\begin{equation}
E - e\sin E = M
\end{equation}
for the eccentric anomaly $E$, then converting
\begin{equation}
\tan\frac{\nu}{2}
=
\sqrt{\frac{1+e}{1-e}}\,
\tan\frac{E}{2}
\end{equation}
\citep{murray1999solar}.
Here $P$ is the orbital period and $t_0$ is a time of periastron passage.
Supplementary notation, unit conventions, and the visualization equations are
collected in Appendix~\ref{app:notation}.

For an edge-on orbit ($\sin i = 1$), the semi-amplitude is related to the
component masses and period by
\begin{equation}
K
=
\left(\frac{2\pi G}{P}\right)^{1/3}
\frac{M_p}{(M_\star + M_p)^{2/3}}
\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-e^2}},
\label{eq:K}
\end{equation}
where $G$ is Newton's constant, $i$ is the orbital inclination to the sky
plane, $M_\star$ is the stellar mass, and $M_p$ is the planet mass.
When evaluating Eq.~\eqref{eq:K} numerically we convert $P$ to seconds so that
the combination is dimensionally consistent in SI units. Given $K$, $P$, $e$,
and $M_\star$, we solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:K} for $M_p$ and obtain the relative
orbital semi-major axis from Kepler's third law,
\begin{equation}
a^3 = \frac{G(M_\star + M_p)}{4\pi^2}\,P^2.
\end{equation}
When $\sin i < 1$, the mass inferred from Eq.~\eqref{eq:K} under the edge-on
assumption is interpreted as the minimum mass $M_p\sin i$; the general
amplitude formula appears in Appendix~\ref{app:notation}.

\subsection{Parameter estimation}

Let $\{t_j, v_j, \sigma_j\}$ denote the observed times, radial velocities, and
uncertainties. We minimize the weighted residual sum of squares
\begin{equation}
\chi^2(\boldsymbol{\theta})
=
\sum_j
\left(
\frac{v_j - v_r(t_j;\boldsymbol{\theta})}{\sigma_j}
\right)^2,
\end{equation}
with parameter vector
$\boldsymbol{\theta} = (K, P, e, \omega, t_0, \gamma)$.

Because $\chi^2$ is nonconvex in the orbital elements, we first estimate a
dominant period with a normalized Lomb--Scargle periodogram
\citep{virtanen2020scipy} and then perform a global search with differential
evolution \citep{storn1997differential,virtanen2020scipy}, which maintains a
population of candidate solutions and updates them by mutation and crossover.
The best candidate is then polished with a bounded nonlinear least-squares
solver \citep{virtanen2020scipy}. For the observational series the period
search window is centered on the periodogram peak; eccentricity is restricted
to $0 \le e \le 0.85$ to avoid near-parabolic degeneracies. Numerical settings
and per-series bounds are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:optimizer}. For the
synthetic validation run we also record the best-so-far $\chi^2$ after each
differential-evolution generation (Figure~\ref{fig:convergence}).

\subsection{Orbit visualization}

After fitting, we construct barycentric initial conditions consistent with the
derived $a$, $e$, and component masses, and integrate the Newtonian two-body
problem with the velocity Verlet algorithm \citep{verlet1967computer}. This
integration is used solely for visualization of the reflex orbit; it is not
part of the likelihood evaluation. The explicit initial-condition and update
equations are given in Appendix~\ref{app:notation}.

\section{Data}

\subsection{Synthetic validation set}

To quantify recovery accuracy we generate a synthetic RV series for a
Jupiter-class companion around a $\sim 1.08\,M_\odot$ star with planted
parameters
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
P &= 147.5\,\mathrm{d},\quad
K \approx 30.5\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}},\quad
e = 0.19,\\
\omega &= 1.05\,\mathrm{rad},\quad
t_0 = 18.0\,\mathrm{d},\quad
\gamma = 4.5\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}},\quad
M_p = 0.82\,M_J.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
Observation times are drawn irregularly over a $420\,\mathrm{d}$ window.
Independent Gaussian measurement noise of standard deviation
$\sigma_{\mathrm{n}} = 2.2\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ and an independent Gaussian
jitter draw of standard deviation
$\sigma_{\mathrm{jit}} = 0.7\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ are added to each sample
(Appendix~\ref{app:notation}).

  \item HD~3651 \citep{butler2006catalog}, archive file
    \texttt{UID\_0003093\_RVC\_002.tbl}.
\end{itemize}
Each series provides Julian dates, barycentric radial velocities in
$\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, and published per-point uncertainties. Host masses

that the first observation is at $t=0$, leaving the relative cadence unchanged.
Working CSV copies and a machine-readable provenance catalog are packaged under
\path{data/nasa_exoplanet_archive/}.

\paragraph{Data provenance.}
All observational inputs used here are public NASA Exoplanet Archive RVC
products downloaded from the archive bulk/time-series service. Original IPAC
table headers retain the literature reference, telescope, and instrument
metadata; those identifiers are preserved in \path{catalog.json} so each fit
can be traced to a specific archive file and paper.

\section{Discussion}

The synthetic experiment confirms that differential evolution followed by
local refinement is sufficient to recover the dominant Keplerian parameters
when the noise is Gaussian and the sampling covers multiple orbital cycles
\citep{storn1997differential,wright2009efficient}. Eccentricity is the most
weakly constrained element in our synthetic run, as expected when the
signal-to-noise ratio is modest and the true $e$ is moderate
\citep{ford2005quantifying}.

Across the observational sample, 51~Peg and HD~209458 behave like compact,
low-eccentricity hot-Jupiter signals of the kind first revealed by early Doppler
surveys \citep{mayor1995jupiter,marcy1997peg}, while 70~Vir and HD~3651
illustrate longer-period eccentric recoveries with acceptable residuals
\citep{marcy1996planet,butler2006catalog}. Several caveats apply to all four
fits. First, although published uncertainties are used for weighting, residual
systematics and stellar jitter are not modeled explicitly, so formal $\chi^2$
values should not be over-interpreted. Second, the masses we report are
$M_p\sin i$; without an independent inclination constraint the true masses may
be larger. Third, the model assumes a single planet and a constant systemic
velocity, omitting stellar activity, instrumental offsets between instruments,
and additional companions \citep{fischer2014exoplanet}. The present solutions
are therefore best viewed as reproducible single-Keplerian pipeline validation
on named public archive series rather than as replacement orbital ephemerides.

Natural extensions include periodogram-based period initialization
\citep{cumming2004detectability}, multi-planet Keplerian models
\citep{wright2009efficient}, and Bayesian posterior sampling for full
uncertainty quantification \citep{ford2005quantifying}.

\section{Conclusions}

We have described a Keplerian radial-velocity pipeline for single-planet
parameter estimation. The method combines an analytic RV forward model,
Lomb--Scargle period initialization, differential-evolution global search, and
least-squares refinement, with derived planet mass and semi-major axis under
the edge-on limit. Synthetic tests recover $K$ and $P$ to within a few percent.
Application to four public NASA Exoplanet Archive series recovers
literature-consistent short-period circular solutions for 51~Peg and HD~209458
together with longer-period eccentric solutions for 70~Vir and HD~3651. The
accompanying open-source implementation provides a reproducible basis for
further methodological development.

\appendix

\section{Notation and supplementary definitions}
\label{app:notation}

\subsection*{Symbols}

Unless noted otherwise, times are expressed in days in the fitting interface and
converted to seconds only when evaluating Newtonian formulas that involve $G$.
Masses $M_\odot$ and $M_J$ denote one solar mass and one Jupiter mass,
respectively. The Doppler observables use rest wavelength $\lambda$ and
observed wavelength shift $\Delta\lambda$, so that
$z=\Delta\lambda/\lambda$. The fitted velocities $v_j$ are identified with the
line-of-sight speed $v$ obtained from $z$.

The orbital inclination $i$ is the angle between the orbital angular-momentum
vector and the line of sight, so that an edge-on orbit has $i=90^\circ$ and
$\sin i = 1$. The relative orbital semi-major axis $a$ is the semi-major axis
of the planet--star separation vector.

\subsection*{General semi-amplitude}

Allowing for arbitrary inclination, Eq.~\eqref{eq:K} generalizes to
\begin{equation}
K
=
\left(\frac{2\pi G}{P}\right)^{1/3}
\frac{M_p\sin i}{(M_\star + M_p)^{2/3}}
\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-e^2}},
\label{eq:K-general}
\end{equation}
with $P$ in seconds when $G$ is in SI units. Inverting Eq.~\eqref{eq:K} with
$\sin i = 1$ therefore returns $M_p\sin i$ whenever the true inclination is
unknown.

\subsection*{Residual diagnostics}

For $N$ observations the unweighted residual root-mean-square reported in the
tables is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RMS}
=
\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}\bigl(v_j - v_r(t_j;\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})\bigr)^2},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ is the best-fit parameter vector. This is
distinct from the weighted objective $\chi^2(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ used during
optimization. Relative errors in validation tables are
$|\hat{q}-q_{\mathrm{true}}|/|q_{\mathrm{true}}|$ for a scalar quantity $q$.

\subsection*{Synthetic noise model}

Synthetic observations are generated as
\begin{equation}
v_j
=
v_r(t_j;\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\mathrm{true}})
+
\varepsilon_{\mathrm{n},j}
+
\varepsilon_{\mathrm{jit},j},
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{n},j}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\mathrm{n}}^2)$ and
$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{jit},j}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\mathrm{jit}}^2)$ are
independent. The fitting weights use
$\sigma_j = \sigma_{\mathrm{n}}$ and therefore do not include the jitter
variance. For the public archive series we use the published per-point
uncertainties supplied with each RVC file.

\subsection*{Orbit visualization}

Orbit panels show barycentric trajectories in an orbital $x$--$z$ plane with
the line-of-sight direction along $+z$. Given relative polar radius
\begin{equation}
r = \frac{a(1-e^2)}{1+e\cos\nu}
\end{equation}
and argument of periastron $\omega$, the planet--star relative state in that
plane is obtained by rotating the perifocal vectors through $\omega$.
Barycentric positions and velocities then follow from the mass ratio
$\mu_p = M_p/(M_\star+M_p)$,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{r}_\star = -\mu_p\,\mathbf{r},\qquad
\mathbf{r}_p = (1-\mu_p)\,\mathbf{r},
\end{equation}
and likewise for the velocities. Starting from those initial conditions, the
Newtonian accelerations
\begin{equation}
\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_\star
=
G M_p\,\frac{\mathbf{r}_p-\mathbf{r}_\star}{|\mathbf{r}_p-\mathbf{r}_\star|^3},
\qquad
\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_p
=
-G M_\star\,\frac{\mathbf{r}_p-\mathbf{r}_\star}{|\mathbf{r}_p-\mathbf{r}_\star|^3}
\end{equation}
are integrated with the velocity Verlet update
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{r}(t+\Delta t)
&=
\mathbf{r}(t)+\mathbf{v}(t)\,\Delta t+\tfrac12\mathbf{a}(t)\,(\Delta t)^2,\\
\mathbf{v}(t+\Delta t)
&=
\mathbf{v}(t)+\tfrac12\bigl(\mathbf{a}(t)+\mathbf{a}(t+\Delta t)\bigr)\Delta t.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The visualization uses a fixed number of steps over one fitted orbital period
and does not enter the RV likelihood.

\section{Optimizer configuration}
\label{app:optimizer}

The global stage uses SciPy's differential-evolution routine with
\texttt{polish=False}, \texttt{updating=deferred}, a fixed random seed, and
at most $300$ iterations for the synthetic run ($400$ for the observational
series). The local stage is SciPy's bounded nonlinear least-squares solver
applied to the weighted residual vector, with a budget of
$2\times 10^4$ function evaluations. The free parameter vector is ordered as
$(K, P, e, \omega, t_0, \gamma)$.

Figure~\ref{fig:convergence} shows the synthetic-run convergence trace: the
best population $\chi^2$ after each differential-evolution generation, the
final least-squares polish, and the relative errors in $K$ and $P$ against the
planted truth. Early generations reduce $\chi^2$ by orders of magnitude; once
$P$ is approximately recovered, further improvement is comparatively gradual
before the local polish.

\section{Reproducibility commands}
\label{app:repro}

From the project root, regenerate the CSV tables and light-theme vector PDFs,
then compile this manuscript, with
\begin{verbatim}
MPLBACKEND=Agg uv run python examples/export_paper_figures.py
latexmk -pdf -cd docs/exoplanet_showcase_report.tex
\end{verbatim}
Print figures in the PDF are drawn natively with \textsc{PGFPlots} from the
exported CSV tables under \path{docs/figures/plotdata/}. The same export script
also writes Matplotlib PDF copies under \path{docs/figures/}.
